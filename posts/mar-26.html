limits

recently, it feels like a lot of loose threads have started to come together. things are beginning to form something larger, something that almost resembles a bigger picture. i could easily blame this on being at the end of my bachelor's degree - say that i've simply learned enough to start understanding what's going on -, but the truth is that most of it has happened over the past year, and most of it is a result of my choices.

from when i started considering vienna, things have been flowing and connecting almost too quickly.
i've been introduced to so so many new concepts, and i've found niches i want to bury myself in, corners i want to explore and populate and futures i want to live.

from within the mess of hunger that is knowledge, here are some of the things that stuck:

langphil

first of all, i've realized that i'm very interested in the "philosophy of language" side of logic!! wittgenstein's tractatus is one of my favourite books (it's kind of everywhere in this blog), but i absolutely want to learn more about the other philosophers who shaped the history of semantics and the search for meaning.

to get closer to the topic - which isn't exactly part of my cs curriculum - and to make sure i was actually interested in the subject itself and not just the idea of it, i read "a very brief introduction to the philosophy of language", by paolo casalegno. it's an introduction to an introduction — not particularly technical, nor especially deep - but it confirmed that i am, indeed, very interested!!

i've always been drawn to "meta" things. the idea of "stepping outside" of our minds and looking at ourselves from an external perspective has fascinated me for as long as i can remember. it's one of the aspects i find most compelling about the tractatus - the claim that language cannot be used to describe itself. how can we, so constrained by our language, by our means, by our minds, understand it, when we're so deeply embedded in it? how could a metalanguage exist without collapsing into just another language?
(it is, in a way, gödel's second incompleteness theorem: $T \not\vdash Consis(T)$, a system cannot prove its own consistency).

more generally, the inescapable reality of being bounded by our perception is one of the topics i've always found most interesting.

i think one of the views on this that has had the most impact on me was expressed by parmenides in his discussion of the "is not" (or at least, that's what i took from it).

parmenides says:

"the other, namely, that it is not, and that it must needs not be—that, i tell thee, is a path that none can learn of at all. for thou canst not know what is not—that is impossible—nor utter it;"

he defines, in opposition to the "is" (which is essentially Truth), something that acts as a limit. he gets, in my opinion, terribly close to wittgenstein's:

"3.02 the thought contains the possibility of the state of affairs which it thinks. what is thinkable is also possible."
"4.1212 what can be shown cannot be said."
"5.6 the limits of my language mean the limits of my world."
"5.632 the subject does not belong to the world but is a limit of the world."

thought and being, language and reality - all collapse into one single thing. we cannot escape the bounds in which our thoughts operate; we cannot "not think" of something without, in some way, bringing it into existence. thinking and being become intertwined - if something can be thought, it must exist in some capacity. ("nothingness" remains fundamentally out of reach)

whenever i think too much about what "reality" is, i inevitably slip into some form of solipsism, or into a chain of thought that leads to radical uncertainty. i don't conclude that i am the only "real" person alive, or fall into a nihilist slope of non-sense - i get deeply fascinated by just how limited we are as beings, but at the same time, how impossible it is to imagine what it would mean to be otherwise.

i am particularly weak to these sorts of topics because of the way my memory works (or rather sorta doesn't?) - i remember things not as events or pictures, but as facts, as if told to me by someone; i have no recollection of myself in the past (nor a concept of the passage of time, really), and i basically reconstruct my feelings or actions from likelihood (based on the knowledge i have of myself). because of it, i spend a lot of time in what romans call "pippe mentali" (mental jerkoffs) about memory, time, and especially perception - which inevitably pulls me back towards philosophers.

so, the study of meaning deeply fascinates me. but alongside it, the study of patterns, generators, and structures does as well. language is one of the most pervasive elements of our world (and i do agree with wittgenstein in saying that it defines us), but how is it actually structured? is it somehow innate? how much of it is shaped by lived experience? how much does language shape our experience, versus experience shaping language?
i obviously find the sapir-whorf hypothesis very fascinating - not in its strong, deterministic form, but still. i can't help but think that, in a life so deeply filtered by language (in our thoughts, in our communication), it's impossible for it not to influence the way we think.
i watched the lea boroditsky TED talk and also listened to the lingthusiasm podcast episode about sapir-whorf, and i'd really like to read the original texts sometime soon.

MSO, finite state machines and regular expressions all three of these ways of describing patterns are equivalent (intertranslatable) these different ways of thinking about patterns are the same i think that there is some sort of object of study, there is a realness to the pattern that can be classified any of these ways there's some essence of patterns that can be described this way (very seemingly different ways) constructive logics vs classical logics curry-howard-lambek (i want to get into category theory !!)
